: Squats in Berlin :

Berlin’s squats represent a significant piece of the city’s cultural and political history. Following the eviction of Liebigstraße 34, public attention returned to the city’s last few remaining occupied houses. These squats have long been symbols of resistance against gentrification and strongholds for left-wing activism. The squatter movement began in the 1970s, especially in Kreuzberg, where activists preserved old, vacant buildings that were slated for demolition. After the fall of the Berlin Wall, squatting expanded eastward, with new scenes forming in areas like Mitte, Friedrichshain, and Prenzlauer Berg. Today, Berlin’s most notable squats offer a glimpse into the city’s rich, rebellious history and social movements [Source: Jacek Slaski, "Squats in Berlin: The Most Famous Occupied Houses," published on October 12, 2020.
